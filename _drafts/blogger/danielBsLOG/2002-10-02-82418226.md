<br />
<br /><i>Manifesto (in progress): When we talk about "the system," it sounds like warmed-over Marx. But then, who can deny that there is "a system"? Am I right? So, the system. The system can absorb almost every act of protest. Violence, for example. The system absorbs violence quite nicely, thank you. Almost without a bump. Still. The system is very poorly equipped to handle certain changes. Such as: it doesn't cope well with less. That is, people who ask less, not more, of the system. People who do not contribute to "growth." Who don't go to the doctor that often, or take in a lot of movies, or consider a three-blade razor an improvement on a two-blade. 
<br />
<br />The act of asking for less, not more, is a radical act--far more radical than it's ever given credit for. If you don't believe me, ask yourself why, after modern history's most appalling act of terrorism, the President of the United States of America would tell his people to keep on shopping or the terrorists win. Here's why: consumption keeps the system standing. Take away consumption, and you need to come up with a radically different system. That said, dropping out and joining a commune is volunteering to put your ideas in quarantine. And besides, communes tend to be a bit heavy on the rules. A tad monolithic. We do not live in communes because we refuse to cede the culture. We prefer to be within the culture, a disturbance. We are in the process of imagining the next civilization while living within the existing one...</i> 
<br />
<br />by James MacKinnon. You'll have to buy the mag to read the rest of it (that article is not online)...
