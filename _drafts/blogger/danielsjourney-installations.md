# 2001-09-24 ...

Here is the introduction to "hate mail," for those coming at this ahead of time. If you don't want to spoil any part of the installation, then read no further of this post!

"Abuse" isn't the right word, but it's the first one that comes to mind.

See Also: Confusion

See Also: Horror

See Also: Tears

See Also: Hate

This work began as a response to the first hate mail Miriam or I have ever received. I'm sure it will not be the last, however as the first it had a great affect on us.

Three days later, on September 11 2001, the most terrible act of my lifetime occurred in New York City and Washington D.C.

Without knowing how to respond to these acts of abuse and hatred on both a micro and a macro scale, I immediately searched for meaning.

I'm convinced there is none.

This work, therefore, is about that struggle to find meaning in the face of both small and inconceivably large pain.

There is no universal meaning for abuse, hate, terrorism, bigotry. There are only small connections we can make each day that keep scraps of sanity in our cupboards of life.

This work is about my attempt at a few small connections

# 2001-09-24 ...

Here's the original postlude:

Interesting things happened during the making of this work. I set out one Sunday afternoon to find subjects, as my work with film is primarily about the subject matter and connections with meaning. I look for juxtapositions, hidden beauties, subtle connections, textures, integrations...

I found that the area I live in, South Florida (I've decided to capitalize the South because it deserves it), is so washed out, that there is nothing strikingly beautiful, nor strikingly "not" beautiful. Even the construction (and there is a lot of it) is done in a way to point strongly to the "new and improved"...there is nothing really old here, and even if there were, they wouldn't let it stay that way. I live in a world of newer, bigger, faster, hotter to the extreme.

Textures are forgone for clean angles; beautiful age is forgone for fresh paint, another face job; the new has no time for the old, the old not enough time to keep up with the new.

So in responding to a letter which reeked of not just capitalism, but materialism and chauvinism, I found it ironic that I live in an area so strong in all of the above. An area that is so skilled in their ways, that nothing is left to even mock it.

Adapt or die.

I think I'm dead.

So the work became a true integration of two trips into my world, a trip to another world (NJ), and what I could make for myself, thrown together with whatever has been around me, complicated terribly by the events of September 11.

Perhaps you have been able to construct some meaning from this, but if not, please accept my encouragement to create your own meanings for the tragic events of our lives. Just don't implicate me. I'm guilty already.

>Works Echo Differently When Life Overtakes Art
>
>September 25, 2001
>
>By HOLLAND COTTER
>
>
>
>
>Does contemporary art have an impact on everyday life? The
>matter is up for debate. There's no question, though, that
>life can transform the way we look at new art.
>
>An overwhelming political or social crisis - AIDS, for
>example - can alter aesthetic taste. Art that seemed smart
>and zeitgeisty one day feels out of sync the next.
>Hard-to-categorize work suddenly makes total sense. Fresh
>images, styles and attitudes emerge to deal with current
>events.
>
>With the destruction of the World Trade Center this dynamic
>went into play. American culture was on instant high alert,
>scrambling both to accommodate what was happening and to
>avoid giving offense. Television shows were rescripted;
>films were pulled from release; Broadway plays discreetly
>dropped bits that might seem insensitive.
>
>By contrast, gallery shows opened pretty much as planned.
>Most art isn't amenable to last-minute editing. And the art
>world resists self-censorship, for good reason. Art
>routinely gets punished for "offenses" - sexual, religious,
>whatever - that the rest of American culture gets away
>with. The only way to deal with this is to hold your
>ground. So even shows made problematic by the recent
>tragedy appeared as originally intended.
>
>One of these was the New York solo debut of the young
>German artist Christoph Draeger, which opened last week at
>Roebling Hall in Williamsburg, Brooklyn. Disaster and
>violence are Mr. Draeger's subjects. In the 1990's he
>visited the sites of plane crashes, explosions and tornados
>and constructed detailed models of the scenes, which he
>photographed or painted.
>
>His obsession with destruction - its unpredictable
>occurrence, the voyeuristic fascination it inspires -
>culminated in a 1999 video titled "Crash," currently on
>view at the Palm Beach Institute of Contemporary Art in
>Florida. It is made up entirely of images of midair
>collisions lifted from from news films, amateur videos and
>Hollywood movies, spliced together into a prolonged
>experience of shattering impact.
>
>The effect isn't all that different from the endless
>television replays of planes striking the twin towers. And
>for anyone who watched the buildings collapse over and over
>again on television, aspects of Mr. Draeger's Roebling Hall
>show may have uncomfortable resonance.
>
>In the center of the gallery sits a burned-out
>camper-trailer, eerily reminiscent of the ruined cars that
>recently littered Lower Manhattan streets. A video running
>inside the trailer delivers a short, brutal narrative: a
>young woman wakes up in the trailer, gets drunk, goes crazy
>and immolates herself. The final sequence, an exterior shot
>of the trailer bursting into clouds of fire and smoke, is
>projected large onto the gallery wall.
>
>All of this was conceived months ago, but time has caught
>up with it. Staged scenes of entrapment and destruction
>have been overtaken by stunning real-time correspondents.
>And when art and life cross paths this way, there is no
>comparison between them. Art can't help but seem at best
>child's play, at worst perverse self-indulgence and part of
>a different, distant, artificial world.
>
>But being part of a different world is important. It
>permits Mr. Draeger to add something more to his work, to
>move it into the realm of metaphor where life and art can
>imaginatively coexist. He has filled three of the gallery
>walls surrounding the incinerated trailer with a
>continuous, floor-to-ceiling video projection of a dense,
>lush rain forest (actually shot at the Bronx Zoo), offering
>a cool, restorative vision of paradisal life rising from
>the ashes.
>
>Other New York artists, and their number is surely growing
>by the day, are making work in direct response to the trade
>center disaster. Within days of the event, the three-man
>team of Gustavo Bonevardi, John Bennett and Robert Hammond
>circulated a proposal on the Internet "for the immediate
>construction of the Manhattan skyline." It involves
>installing 80 high-intensity laser lights near the trade
>center site and projecting silhouettes of the Towers 1 and
>2 as beams of light against the night sky.
>
>The artists Paul Myoda and Julian LaVerdiere came up with a
>similarly utopian proposal, which they titled "Phantom
>Towers." (A computer- manipulated version of it appeared on
>the cover of The New York Times Magazine on Sunday.) They
>have now joined up with Mr. Bonevardi, Mr. Bennett and Mr.
>Hammond in what they are calling the "Tower of Light"
>initiative, sponsored by the nonprofit arts organization
>Creative Time.
>
>Mr. Myoda and Mr. LaVerdiere spent a lot of time in the
>trade center last year. They were participants in an
>international residency program called World Views, which
>under the auspices of the Lower Manhattan Cultural Council
>provided artists with studio space on the 91st floor of
>Tower 1. A participant in this year's program, the
>Jamaican-born artist Michael Richards, was trapped in the
>tower when it collapsed.
>
>At a memorial gathering at the Studio Museum in Harlem on
>Friday, one of his colleagues in the program, Jeff
>Konigsberg, said that Mr. Richards arrived at the 91st
>floor at 9 p.m. on Monday, Sept. 10, planning to work late
>into the night. He was not heard from again. His body was
>identified last week.
>
>Mr. Konigsberg also described the almost-finished
>sculptures in Mr. Richards's trade center studio. They were
>part of a series he had been working on for nearly decade
>based on the theme of the Tuskegee Airmen, a segregated
>unit of African- American pilots who were awarded more than
>150 Flying Crosses for valor during World War II, even
>though, like all blacks in the armed forces at the time,
>they were officially assigned noncombat "support" roles.
>
>Mr. Richards's homage to them often took the form of a
>gilded bronze or steel figure cast from his own body. One
>of these, "Tar Baby vs. St. Sebastian," was exhibited two
>years ago at the Studio Museum. The figure, in a sleek
>flight uniform, has the timeless look of a space traveler,
>standing erect, his face turned upward, his feet several
>inches from the ground, as if he were levitating or
>ascending. In place of the arrows shot at the Christian
>martyr-saint, the airman is pierced with a dozen small
>fighter planes, which seem to both wound him and lift him
>up. Art and life meet in the shadow of tragedy and are made
>larger by the encounter.

For the benefit of those here between Nov 15 and 18th:

"

A photograph that 'may define a generation'

Edward Fine

National Post

The bus arrived at One World Trade Center; I looked at my watch and thought, "Not bad, about an hour and a half, door to door." I walked through the door and headed straight to the reception area for visitors; I knew the routine from my prior visits. I presented my passport, gave the name of the firm and individual I would be seeing. The receptionist called; John Paul was in. My pass was issued immediately. I could go right up.

Could all of this have been different? Yes, I might have made the earlier ferry and John Paul might not have been in yet. There could have been traffic on the city streets and I might have arrived 15 minutes later. Any number of other permutations and combinations could have changed the spot I stood in when the plane hit. A series of seconds and inches interwoven into a pattern of life dictating the future, dissectible, guided.

The door was open, I entered. John Paul came to greet me. His office faced the Hudson; I sat looking out over the river, John Paul his back to it. A small boat, perhaps a ferry, was crossing the river. From the 87th floor, it looked small, everything looked small. I commented that this was the type of day just such an office was made for.

John Paul and I spent some time together, I don't know how much, it didn't seem important. I was early, my next appointment was hours off, I had time to spare. Life seemed to be smiling on me. John Paul introduced me to some colleagues, nice young men, perhaps a little younger then my son. My business was concluded. We shook hands and John Paul accompanied me to the door. I had to use the men's room, John Paul didn't have his key but the receptionist did have one. I took it. I returned the key, made a phone call, got no answer, left a message, altering the time-line of my life by seconds and inches.

Coincidence, I don't think so.

I left the 87th floor office, got the elevator quickly and reached the 78th floor, the changing point for the elevator to the ground floor. I exited the elevator, took a few steps and saw the down elevator waiting. I started to hurry, too late, the door closed. I turned and walked to my right toward another bank of elevators, hoping to find a quick way down. I turned and headed back in the opposite direction toward the windows, looked at the bank of elevators and realized they were not the ones that would take me to the ground floor.

Only 25 to 30 seconds had elapsed since I had missed the earlier elevator. I turned again and walked a few steps, three or four seconds, maybe less, and was opposite an adjoining hallway. I stopped, faced the hallway, heard an explosion, turned to my left and saw a wall of debris and smoke heading for me, heard screams, leaped into the adjoining hallway as the smoke and debris flew by. My first thought was that a bomb had gone off, I was lucky to be standing in front of that hallway. Would there be a fire, unbreathable smoke, floor collapse? The explosion seemed to have been right there on my floor. Wait, I am alive, uninjured, more screams, people emerging from the main corridor screaming and injured or burned, or both. Mass confusion, go into survival mode, must get out of the building, find emergency exit, get some fresh air before being overcome by smoke....

We ran down the steps as fast as we could and as fast as the traffic in front of us would allow. We all knew that time was our enemy. You could smell the smoke. Was there a serious fire, there must be. Focus on moving, don't look back, ignore the pain. Need fresh air. After about 20 floors of moving fast, we started to encounter serious traffic. Why are we stopped, we have to keep moving, smoky smell, bad feeling. Most were calm, one or two start to yell, a brief panic, calm restored. We started to move, making decent time but not great. Seconds seemed like hours, every moment counted. Stopped again, what's going on, why are we stopped? What happened? Moving again, lady handing out wet paper towels, didn't need one, I'm okay. Stopped again, sweating, loosened tie, shock wearing off, we need to move, legs hurting, where are we? Moving again, stopped. Get wet paper towel. Getting warmer, or does it just seem that way. Come on get going, moving, making good time, stopped. Everybody to the right, injured coming down, 30th floor, maybe we will get out in time. Moving, stopped, emergency personnel coming up, real heroes, can't pay them enough, get to right, clear pathway, moving again, 25th floor, air fresher, careful broken glass below, stopped.

....We reach 20th fl., come on not so far to go now, let's move, stopped, more emergency people going up. Moving again, faster, yes, yes 10th fl., air getting cooler, 6th fl., be careful water down below, 5th floor, steps wet, 4th ,water getting deeper, keep moving, not far to go now, 2nd, wetter, fresher air, cool, smelling the outside, inches from freedom. We reach the ground floor, temporary jubilation, no time for celebrating, keep moving, follow directions to exit, legs in agony, want help, no, I can make it, deep water, sprinklers pouring down on us, light, freedom, we are outside. Time to rest, no, keep moving get away from the building, look up, building burning, look down, debris and dirt all over, what happened, could it have been a plane?

One block away, can't walk anymore, must rest, sit, safe now, fresh air, the ordeal is over, must call home and let everyone know I am safe, can't, they need the lines for emergency personnel....

Walking now, ever so slowly, EXPLOSION, look back, building crumbling, frozen, awe struck, RUN someone screams, start running, legs hurt not moving very fast, turn corner get ten feet, look back, large cloud of debris, ash and smoke heading my way, can't outrun it, lie down a voice calls, we lie face down on the street, a priest starts praying, DEATH has arrived for me. We are engulfed, warm ash covering us, breathing extremely difficult, hold your breath, keep your eyes closed, suffocation. I take the wet towel, the one given to me by the lady on the stairs, from my pocket, place it over my nose and mouth and try sucking air through it. It is possible but not enough air, keep trying, a minute goes by, open eyes, big mistake, ash gets in them, close them again, no light anyway, the darkest dark imaginable, nothing. More minutes pass by, can't tell how many, open eyes, light coming through. Maybe I will be saved. More light, café across the street, people are heading for it. I get up and struggle to it and get inside, still don't feel safe but at least there is clean air and fresh water. I sit down and drink. I am covered with an inch or more of ash. I look at other supernatural appearing beings, it strikes me as surreal....

Once more my life has been saved by mere minutes. I rest, and wait for some of the air outside to clear. I must get moving again. Nothing here is safe and the air is unhealthy. I leave the café, Au Bon Pain, thinking of survival and how nice the café's employees were, attending to the injured, providing free everything. New Yorkers are great people, made even more extraordinary when faced with impossible circumstances. The real heroes, police, fireman and rescue workers and the everyday heroes who pitch in without being called. A melting pot of the extraordinary.

I emerge from the café and begin walking uptown. Limping would be more precise. I can't imagine getting back to the Waterway and the ferry in this condition. I have not cleaned myself off, but I see many like me. After several blocks I spot a bus that appears to be loading passengers. Upon reaching the bus, I learn it is heading for the Port Authority. Close enough for me. I board. I call home and after numerous tries, finally get through. I am almost safe. After 20 minutes, the bus finally is able to move. We are on our way. I will live.

God was watching me.

# 2001-11-18 ...

Well, two-thirds in, two days out of three down, one more about to commence, and the maybe-not-so artistic-event-of-the-century has had an interesting history.

Showed up and immediately thought it was going to be a bust. A birthday party had been booked for the room of the restaurant that my show was supposed to be happening. Now, a few tables being occupied is in fact a great thing, but a packed room with drunk people celebrating somebody's 30-whatever-eth birthday is not the proper backdrop for a F_N ART PREMIER! :(  Good news was they moved the party to a different room. :)

Set up was very difficult, but after quite a few hitches, the thing was up and running by 7:15, for my 8:00 opening. Soon enough my constiuency showed, and we had a great time. People interfaced with it as they felt like it. It was a slightly distracting environ for this group of high-thinkers. They look forward to interfacing with it in the relative peace of their homesteads. Also my said constiuency was much smaller than anticipated, and to all of you who blew me off, that's your thing. I'll try not to think bad thoughts about you for too long. No need to perpetuate the karma.

All and all a great opening night. Met a bunch of new, interesting people; a lot of positive vibes about the work. Didn't have too big a headache at 2 am after being at Dada since 3:30.

Last night was less than steller. The crowd at the place was a much different crowd than that of the night prior; and not because my usual crowd wasn't there; the actual entire place had a different vibe. It was much more clematis/south beach club scene types (or maybe wanna be's and that's why there were there). Had Marylin Manson's "The Beautiful People" going through my head a lot of the night. Just the part that goes "(guitar) du-du-du-dom ...the beautiful people, the beautiful people... du-du-du-dom." Read a lot of a decent book. It was an interesting experience, at one point there were people sitting all around me. I couldn't actually move because I was completely cornered (there's no way for you to imagine this if you haven't been to this room in this place, but just go with it). I was reading my book and listening in on two other conversations at once, sort of subconsciously; I wasn't actually following any of the conversations around me, but little phrases and bits were making their way into my ears and distorting the story I was trying to concentrate on in my book. It was cul. :)

Anyway. so far, no more or less than expected. It's been interesting and education and at times fun and harrowing. Cheers.
