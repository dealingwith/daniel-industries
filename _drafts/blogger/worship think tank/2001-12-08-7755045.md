"i have a thing about this ageism, it seems so prevalent in evangelical
<br />thinking, the emphasis on 'young people' having all the answers. it's partly
<br />modernist - new=better, and that 'generations' thing that evangelicals do.
<br />it's partly a failure to realise that it's cultural position that matters.
<br />in our context, the young/old boundary that matters is the modern/postmodern
<br />one loosely speaking, which can perhaps be taken as the pre/post 60s
<br />boundary as well - which is moving further and further up the age scale. the
<br />boundary is now around age 45, but the church as usual hasn't quite caught
<br />up with this, because it created whole patterns of ministry in the 70s when
<br />this boundary was around 25. looking around it seems that most of the people
<br />on the cutting edge are actually in their 30s, partly because it takes a
<br />while to develop the knowledge and self-confidence [and self-knowledge] to
<br />critique the church effectively from within. and also because it's only when
<br />you move beyond the exciting relevant world of youth ministry that the real
<br />plight of the church hits you. sitting in a pew age 30, listening to a
<br />boring sermon, remembering how much better it was when your youth group did
<br />its own worship and wondering why you can't play your own music in church
<br />like you did then..."
<br /><a href="http://www.btinternet.com/~smallritual/">Steve Collins</a>
